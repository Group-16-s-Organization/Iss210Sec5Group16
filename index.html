Roe V. Wade: The History

Roe V. Wade decision was made on January 22, 1973 and legalized abortion throughout the United States. It was left in each state's hands to individually decide the laws regarding abortion for their given state. According to Justice Harry Blackmun, some states were justifying that a fetus is a person and therefore protected under the 14th amendment then the Supreme Court determined that that theory is debatable and can not overpower women’s rights. Since the Roe V. Wade decision, there has been a constant fight between those who are “pro-life” and those who are “pro-choice” in America.

This photo shows the nine justices who voted for or against the overturn of Roe V. Wade. The decision was made to overturn with a 5-4 majority, with the majority of the majority being men. It is argued that the overruling of Roe V. Wade is constitutional due to reproductive rights not being included in the constitution, leaving the legality of abortions up to the individual state governments. Ultimately, women in 13 states could be affected by trigger laws that ban abortion with no exceptions, including incest and rape, and could also affect their access to livesaving abortions as well. This could cause many women to have to travel hours to other states for the procedure, and the criminalization of doctors who do these procedures.

Abortion Bans by State

This image comes from an article by New York Times titled “Tracking the States Where Abortion is Banned”. This map shows U.S. states and the status regarding their laws on abortion. As this map shows, 12 states have fully banned abortion since the overturn of Roe v. Wade. This has made it so that over 35.79 million people with uteruses at this point in time do not have access to abortion in their own state. If you include Georgia, who has banned abortion past 6 weeks, this number becomes 41.19 million. It’s important to note this, because 70% of people who become pregnant don’t find out until 6 weeks or later. This situation is especially detrimental to lower income people who don’t have the time or the money to drive all the way one, two, possibly three states over to receive the care they need.
